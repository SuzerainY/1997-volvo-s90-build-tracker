# Sourcing a Used B6304 Engine for the 960/S90 Build

## Purpose

This guide covers how to find, evaluate, and buy a used Volvo B6304 engine (B6304S, B6304S2, or B6304F) as a build core for Stage 2/Stage 3 work. The strategy: buy a donor engine, mount it on a stand, build it up at your own pace, and swap it into the S90 in a single weekend — keeping the car on the road as a daily driver the entire time.

## Compatible Engine Variants

All of these are White Block inline-6 engines that bolt into the 960/S90:

| Variant | Displacement | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **B6304S** | 2.9L | European-spec, 204 PS. Higher-lift cams than the S2. |
| **B6304S2** | 2.9L | US-spec, 181 hp. Detuned with B6254S cams. Most common in US junkyards. |
| **B6304F** | 2.9L | Found in some 960 model years. Same block architecture. |

Any of these share the same block, head bolt pattern, motor mount locations, and transmission bellhousing. For a full rebuild with forged internals and turbo, the specific variant matters less — you're replacing the internals and cams anyway. The block and head casting are what you're buying.

## Where to Find Them

### Tier 1 — Best Accountability and Selection

1. **Car-Part.com**
   - The junkyard industry's aggregated search engine. Covers thousands of salvage yards across the US.
   - Search for: 1992–1998 Volvo 960 engine, or 1997–1998 Volvo S90/V90 engine.
   - Filter by mileage and distance. Most yards will ship.
   - Call the yard directly to ask questions before buying.

2. **LKQ**
   - Large national chain. Sells pulled and tested engines, usually with 90-day to 6-month warranties.
   - More expensive than self-service yards, but you get some accountability if the engine is junk.

3. **eBay Motors**
   - Search: "B6304," "Volvo 960 engine," "Volvo S90 engine."
   - Look for sellers with high feedback scores (98%+) and clear return policies.
   - Many eBay engine sellers offer 30–90 day warranties.
   - Shipping a long block runs roughly $150–$300 depending on distance.

### Tier 2 — Community Sources

4. **Facebook Groups**
   - "Volvo 960 Parts," "Volvo RWD Parts," "Turbobricks Classifieds."
   - Community sellers often know the history of the engine and the car it came from.
   - You can ask detailed questions and usually get honest answers.

5. **Turbobricks.com Forums — Classifieds Section**
   - The 960 community is small and knowledgeable.
   - If someone is parting out a car, they'll usually tell you exactly what's wrong and what's good.

6. **Craigslist / Facebook Marketplace — Whole Donor Cars**
   - Search for non-running or rough-condition 960/S90/V90 cars.
   - A 960 with a bad transmission, wrecked body, or failed inspection can go for $500–$1,500.
   - You get the engine plus a treasure trove of spare parts (sensors, wiring, brackets, accessories, spare transmission).

### Tier 3 — Self-Service

7. **Pick-n-Pull / Self-Service Junkyards**
   - Cheapest option. You pull the engine yourself.
   - Bring your own tools, engine hoist, and a truck/trailer.
   - No warranty, no guarantees — but the price reflects that.

## The Whole Donor Car Strategy

Buying a complete non-running 960 is often the best value. For $500–$1,500 you get:

- The engine (your build core)
- A spare AW30-40 transmission (useful if yours needs a rebuild at Stage 3)
- All sensors, wiring harnesses, brackets, and accessories
- Spare body panels, interior parts, and trim
- Full knowledge of what car the engine came from

Strip what you need, sell individual parts on eBay and Facebook groups (the 960 community buys parts actively), and scrap the shell. You'll often recoup a significant portion of the purchase price from parts sales.

## Questions to Ask the Seller

Ask these before you buy — whether it's a junkyard, eBay listing, or private seller.

### Critical Questions

| Question | What You Want to Hear | Red Flags |
|---|---|---|
| **Why was the car scrapped/parted out?** | Accident, body rust, transmission failure, electrical problems | Overheating, head gasket failure, oil starvation, timing belt broke |
| **Was the engine running when pulled?** | Yes, ran fine | "Don't know" (normal for junkyards, just means more inspection needed) |
| **What's the mileage?** | Any — lower is nice but condition matters more than miles for a rebuild core | Odometer rollback or "unknown" with no explanation |
| **Any known issues?** | Minor oil leaks, cosmetic issues | Knocking, rod knock, heavy smoke, coolant in oil |
| **Is it a long block or short block?** | Long block (block + head + cams + valve train) | Short block only (means you need to source a head separately) |
| **Is the timing belt intact?** | Yes, belt is on and intact | Belt missing or broken (possible bent valves on this interference engine) |
| **How was it stored?** | Indoors, plugged openings, drained fluids | Sitting outside uncovered for years (moisture intrusion, internal rust) |

### Good-to-Know Questions

- Was the car a single owner? (Suggests more consistent maintenance)
- Do you have any service records? (Rare for a junkyard pull, but worth asking)
- Can you send photos of the engine — especially the valve cover area, spark plugs, and oil cap interior?
- Will you accept a return if the block is cracked or head is warped? (Especially important for eBay/shipped engines)

## Inspecting the Engine

You're buying a build core, not a drop-in runner. You're going to tear it down and rebuild it. So you're looking for a **block that isn't cracked, bores that aren't scored, and a head that isn't warped.** Everything else gets replaced.

### Tools You'll Need

| Tool | Approximate Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| USB bore scope | $30–$50 | Essential. You'll use this on your running car too. |
| Compression tester | $30–$40 | Only usable if you can crank the engine (starter + battery). |
| Leak-down tester | $40–$60 | Alternative to compression test. Works with shop air, no cranking needed. |
| Straight edge (machinist's) | $15–$25 | For checking head deck flatness. |
| Feeler gauge set | $10–$15 | Used with the straight edge. |
| Flashlight | — | A good bright one. |

### Inspection Checklist

Work through this list when you receive the engine or inspect it in person.

#### 1. External Visual Inspection
- [ ] **Look for cracks in the block.** Check around the main bearing caps, cylinder walls (external), and freeze plug areas. Any visible crack = walk away.
- [ ] **Check for signs of overheating.** Discolored or bubbled paint on the block/head, warped or distorted gasket surfaces, white residue around the head gasket line (coolant leak).
- [ ] **Inspect the oil pan and valve cover gasket areas.** Oil seepage is normal and fine. Thick, baked-on sludge everywhere suggests severe neglect.

#### 2. Oil Cap and Valve Cover Interior
- [ ] **Pull the oil cap and look inside with a flashlight.**
  - **Good:** Clean, honey-colored metal. Light varnish. Visible cam lobes without heavy buildup.
  - **Acceptable:** Moderate varnish, some darkening. Normal for a higher-mileage engine.
  - **Bad:** Thick black sludge caked on cam lobes, inside of valve cover, or visible through the oil cap opening. Means the PCV system was neglected and oil changes were skipped. Heavy sludge can mean clogged oil passages — not ideal even for a rebuild.

#### 3. Spark Plug Inspection
- [ ] **Pull all 6 spark plugs and compare them.**
  - **Good:** Even, tan/light brown deposits across all 6. Consistent wear.
  - **Bad:** One plug drastically different from the others — oil-fouled (black/wet), white/blistered (overheating), or heavy carbon. Indicates a cylinder-specific problem (bad rings, cracked valve, head gasket issue on that cylinder).
  - **Note:** Uneven plugs don't necessarily kill the deal for a full rebuild, but they tell you what to expect when you tear down.

#### 4. Bore Scope the Cylinders
- [ ] **Insert the bore scope through each spark plug hole and inspect the cylinder walls.**
  - **Good:** Crosshatch honing marks still visible. Smooth, even surface. No scoring.
  - **Bad:** Deep vertical scratches or scoring (piston ring or wrist pin failure). Heavy ridge at the top of the bore (extreme wear). Pitting or corrosion (moisture intrusion from sitting).
  - **Note:** Light glazing (smooth, shiny walls with faded crosshatch) is normal on higher-mileage engines and is corrected during the rebuild with a hone.

#### 5. Compression Test (If Possible)
- [ ] **Hook up a battery and starter, crank the engine with a compression tester in each cylinder hole.**
  - **Good:** All 6 cylinders within ~10% of each other. Exact PSI matters less than consistency.
  - **Example:** 150, 155, 148, 152, 150, 153 = healthy, even compression.
  - **Bad:** One or two cylinders significantly lower than the rest (e.g., 150, 150, 150, 90, 150, 150). Indicates valve or ring problems in those cylinders.
  - **Note:** If you can't crank the engine (no starter/wiring), skip to the leak-down test.

#### 6. Leak-Down Test (Alternative to Compression)
- [ ] **With each piston at TDC on its compression stroke, apply shop air through the spark plug hole and measure how much leaks past.**
  - **Good:** Less than 10% leak-down across all cylinders.
  - **Acceptable:** 10–20% on a higher-mileage engine.
  - **Bad:** Over 20%, or one cylinder drastically worse than the others.
  - **Listen for where the air escapes:** intake (intake valve not sealing), exhaust (exhaust valve not sealing), crankcase/dipstick tube (rings not sealing), or coolant bubbling (head gasket).

#### 7. Head Deck Flatness
- [ ] **Lay a machinist's straight edge across the head deck surface (where the head gasket sits) in multiple directions.**
  - **Good:** No visible gap between straight edge and deck. Feeler gauge can't fit under it (less than 0.002").
  - **Acceptable:** Up to 0.002" — a machine shop can resurface this easily.
  - **Bad:** Over 0.004" — the head was overheated and severely warped. A machine shop may be able to save it, but it's risky. Ideally, find a better core.

#### 8. Crank Rotation
- [ ] **Turn the crankshaft by hand using a breaker bar on the crank bolt (clockwise, direction of normal rotation).**
  - **Good:** Smooth, even resistance throughout the rotation. You'll feel compression building and releasing on each cylinder — that's normal.
  - **Bad:** Grinding, catching, sudden hard spots, or metallic scraping. Indicates bearing damage, bent rod, or debris.
  - **Note:** If the timing belt is off or broken, DO NOT rotate the crank — on this interference engine, the pistons can contact the valves if the cams aren't in sync.

#### 9. Coolant Passages
- [ ] **Look into the water jacket openings (visible at the head gasket surface and thermostat housing area).**
  - **Good:** Clean aluminum, light mineral deposits.
  - **Acceptable:** Moderate scale or discoloration. Normal for an older engine. You'll clean everything during the build.
  - **Bad:** Heavy brown/orange rust, thick calcium deposits, or green corrosion. Suggests the cooling system was neglected or wrong coolant was used. The block may still be usable, but plan for thorough chemical cleaning.

#### 10. Timing Belt Status
- [ ] **Check if the timing belt is still installed and intact.**
  - **Belt present and intact:** Good sign. The engine likely wasn't killed by a belt failure.
  - **Belt missing or broken:** This is an interference engine. Assume bent valves until proven otherwise during teardown. Not a deal-breaker for a full rebuild (you're replacing valves anyway if doing head work), but factor it into your price negotiation.

## Decision Framework

Use this to decide whether a specific engine is worth buying:

**Buy it if:**
- The car was scrapped for non-engine reasons (accident, rust, transmission, electrical)
- The oil cap interior is clean or lightly varnished
- Bore scope shows crosshatch still visible, no scoring
- Compression/leak-down is even across all cylinders (if testable)
- Head deck is flat (under 0.002")
- Crank rotates smoothly
- Price is right for the condition

**Negotiate hard if:**
- Timing belt is missing or broken (assume bent valves)
- Moderate sludge inside (more cleaning work)
- Can't verify compression or leak-down (more risk)
- Cosmetic issues (surface rust, missing accessories — these don't matter for a rebuild)

**Walk away if:**
- Cracked block (visible external cracks near main caps or cylinders)
- Severely warped head (over 0.004")
- Deep cylinder scoring visible on bore scope
- Evidence of severe overheating (warped surfaces, white residue everywhere)
- Heavy internal rust from moisture intrusion (sat outside uncovered with open ports)
- Seller can't or won't answer basic questions about why the car was scrapped

## After You Buy — First Steps

1. **Mount on engine stand.** Bolts to the bellhousing flange or block.
2. **Drain all fluids.** Oil, coolant remnants.
3. **External cleaning.** Degrease the outside so you can see what you're working with.
4. **Full teardown and inspection.** Disassemble completely. Measure everything — bore diameter, bearing clearances, head flatness, valve condition.
5. **Send the head to a machine shop** for inspection, pressure testing, valve job, and porting (Stage 2).
6. **Send the block to a machine shop** for cleaning, bore measurement, and honing (or overbore if needed for forged pistons).
7. **Order your build parts** based on machine shop measurements — forged pistons, rods, bearings, gaskets, turbo components.
8. **Assemble at your own pace.** No commute deadline. No time pressure.
9. **Swap into the S90 in one weekend** when the build is complete.
