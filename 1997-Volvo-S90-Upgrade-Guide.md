# The Complete DIY Build Guide: 1997 Volvo S90 — From Daily Driver to Dignified Sleeper

*A stage-by-stage breakdown for the home mechanic who wants more out of Volvo's last rear-wheel-drive sedan.*

---

## Meet the Platform

The 1997 Volvo S90 is one of the last of a dying breed — a rear-wheel-drive, inline-six Volvo sedan built on the 960 platform. Under the hood sits the **B6304S**, a 2.9-liter naturally aspirated inline-six from Volvo's "White Block" modular engine family. It's smooth, understressed, and surprisingly tunable for what most people write off as a grandpa car.

**Factory Specs:**

| Spec | Value |
|---|---|
| Engine | B6304S — 2.9L Inline-6 (NA) |
| Horsepower | 181 hp @ 5,200 rpm |
| Torque | 199 lb-ft @ 4,100 rpm |
| Transmission | AW30-43 4-Speed Automatic |
| Drivetrain | Rear-Wheel Drive |
| 0–60 mph | ~8.8–9.1 seconds |
| Fuel Economy | 16–18 city / 24–26 highway |
| Curb Weight | ~3,400 lbs |

The B6304S is an interference engine — meaning if the timing belt snaps, the valves meet the pistons and your day gets very expensive. That detail matters a lot for what comes next.

## Stage 0: The Foundation — Maintenance First, Mods Later

You just picked this car up at 133,000 miles. Before you even think about adding power, you need to make sure the bones are solid. A modified engine on a neglected platform is just a fast way to the junkyard. This is the most important stage, and skipping it will undermine everything that follows.

### Timing Belt Service ✅ (Completed)

The previous owners never touched the timing belt, and at 133k on an interference engine that was a ticking time bomb. This one's done now — the big-ticket reliability item is behind us.

**Completed:**

- ✅ Timing belt (replaced)
- ✅ Hydraulic belt tensioner (replaced)
- ✅ Idler pulley/roller (replaced)
- ✅ Tensioner roller (replaced)
- ✅ Water pump (replaced — smart move doing it while the belt was off)

**Still on the shelf / to-do:**

- ⬜ **Thermostat and housing** — Parts are in hand but not yet installed. This is a quick job on its own and doesn't require pulling the timing belt again. The thermostat housing is accessible from the front of the engine. Worth doing soon — a stuck-open thermostat means the engine runs cool and rich, and a stuck-closed one means overheating. Fresh is best.

**DIY difficulty:** Moderate-hard. This is a 4–6 hour job your first time. The key is getting the timing marks aligned correctly — crankshaft pulley mark to oil pump housing, camshaft pulley marks to the timing belt mounting plate. Rotate the crank clockwise only. Double- and triple-check alignment before you button it up.

**Estimated parts cost:** $150–$300 for a complete kit (IPD, FCP Euro, and Rock Auto all carry kits for this engine).

### Brakes

- New rotors and pads all around (EBC Greenstuff pads are a solid daily-driver upgrade over stock)
- Flush brake fluid with DOT 4
- Inspect brake lines for cracking — replace with stainless steel braided lines for better pedal feel and longevity
- Rebuild or replace calipers if they're sticking

### Cooling System Overhaul

The cooling system on these cars is 27+ years old. Plastic and rubber degrade. A cooling failure at highway speed can warp the head on an aluminum engine.

- Replace all coolant hoses (upper, lower, heater core hoses, expansion tank hoses)
- New expansion tank (the plastic becomes brittle and cracks — this is a known failure point)
- Flush and refill with Volvo-spec coolant (blue/green phosphate-free)
- Inspect the radiator — if it's original, consider a new one. Aftermarket aluminum units are available and flow better

### Ignition System Refresh

At 133k, the ignition system is likely tired. A fresh ignition setup will recover lost power and smooth out the idle.

- Spark plugs (NGK or Bosch Platinum, gapped to spec)
- Ignition coil (the B6304S uses a coil pack — replace if you see any misfires)
- Distributor cap and rotor (if applicable to your specific variant)
- Spark plug wires (Bougicord or equivalent OEM-quality set)

### Fuel System Cleanup

- New fuel filter (inline, under the car — easy swap)
- Fuel injector cleaning or replacement (Bosch OEM injectors are the go-to)
- Inspect fuel pressure regulator for leaks
- Clean the throttle body and idle air control valve

### Suspension and Chassis Refresh

The multilink rear suspension on the 960/S90 platform is actually quite sophisticated, but rubber bushings deteriorate over decades.

- Replace all control arm bushings (front and rear)
- New front struts and rear shocks (Bilstein B4 OEM-replacement is a great baseline, or Bilstein HD for a slightly firmer ride)
- New strut mounts and bump stops
- Inspect and replace sway bar end links and bushings
- New tie rod ends and ball joints (inspect first — replace if any play)
- Alignment after all suspension work

### Transmission Service

The AW30-43 is a durable unit, but it needs love.

- Drain and refill transmission fluid (Volvo spec or equivalent Dexron III/Mercon)
- Replace the transmission filter and pan gasket if accessible
- Inspect the transmission mount — replace if cracked or sagging

### Miscellaneous

- PCV system — clean or replace the PCV valve and associated hoses (oil sludge prevention)
- Vacuum hoses — replace any cracked or brittle vacuum lines (these cause idle issues and check engine lights)
- Motor mounts and transmission mount — replace if torn (you'll feel a noticeable improvement in NVH)
- All accessory belts (serpentine/V-belts)

**After Stage 0, you should have a car that runs like it did when it left the factory — or better.** Smooth idle, no misfires, solid cooling, tight suspension, and confidence that the bottom end isn't about to grenade. This is your foundation.

## Stage 1: Breathing Mods — Targeting ~200–210 hp

Now we're talking. The B6304S responds well to bolt-on breathing modifications. The goal here is to let the engine inhale and exhale more efficiently without cracking it open. These are all bolt-on, DIY-friendly mods that you can do in your driveway over a weekend or two.

**Realistic power target: 195–210 hp, with a noticeably broader torque curve.**

### Intake Side

**Airbox Modification / Panel Filter**

The stock airbox is restrictive. You have two options:

1. **Drill and smooth the stock airbox** — Open up the inlet holes, smooth any casting flash inside the box, and drop in a high-flow panel filter (K&N or equivalent). This is free and effective. The stock airbox already provides good hot-air isolation from the engine bay, so don't throw it away for a cone filter that sucks in hot air.
2. **High-flow panel filter** — A drop-in replacement for the stock paper filter. Simple, cheap, and worth a few horsepower by reducing intake restriction.

**Intake Manifold Inspection and Gasket**

The stock intake manifold gasket can degrade and cause vacuum leaks. While you're in there, inspect the manifold for carbon buildup and clean it out. A fresh gasket ensures a proper seal.

### Exhaust Side

**Performance Exhaust Header/Manifold**

The stock exhaust manifold on the B6304S is cast iron and fairly restrictive. A tubular steel header (if you can source one — they're not common for this platform) or a ported stock manifold will improve exhaust scavenging and free up a few horsepower.

**Cat-Back Exhaust**

Replace the stock exhaust from the catalytic converter back with a **2.5-inch mandrel-bent system**. Don't go bigger than 2.5 inches on a naturally aspirated 2.9L — you'll lose exhaust velocity and actually hurt low-end torque.

- A quality muffler (Magnaflow, Borla, or a custom stainless setup) will reduce backpressure while keeping the car civilized for daily driving
- Keep the catalytic converter for emissions compliance, but consider a **high-flow sports catalyst** if your area allows it — this removes a major restriction while keeping things legal

**DIY note:** A cat-back exhaust is a straightforward job with a lift or jack stands, a sawzall, and some patience with rusty bolts. Budget for penetrating oil and possibly a torch.

### ECU Tuning

The B6304S runs Bosch Motronic engine management. The stock ECU is conservative from the factory.

- **Piggyback ECU** — Units like the Apexi S-AFC or similar can adjust fuel maps on top of the stock ECU. This lets you lean out or enrich the mixture to match your intake and exhaust mods.
- **Aftermarket standalone ECU** — A Megasquirt or similar standalone ECU gives you full control over ignition timing, fuel delivery, and more. This is a bigger project but opens the door to serious tuning later. Many B6304S owners have gone this route successfully.
- **Motronic 4.4 swap** — Some owners have swapped from the Motronic 4.3 ECU to the 4.4 for better tuning flexibility. Worth researching if you want to stay closer to OEM.

A proper tune on a B6304S with intake and exhaust work can net you around **10–15% over stock** on a naturally aspirated setup.

### Fast Road Camshaft

This is the single biggest mechanical modification you can make at Stage 1. A **fast road camshaft** with slightly more aggressive duration and lift than stock will broaden the power band and add meaningful horsepower in the mid-to-upper RPM range.

- Look for a cam profile that adds duration without making the idle lumpy — you're daily driving this car
- A cam with ~10–15 degrees more duration on both intake and exhaust is a good starting point
- You may need to source a regrind from a local cam specialist if off-the-shelf options are limited for the B6304S
- **This job requires the timing belt to come off**, so if you're doing Stage 0 and Stage 1 close together, plan to do the cam swap during the timing belt service

**After Stage 1, you should be sitting around 200–210 hp with a noticeably stronger mid-range pull.** The car will feel more alive above 3,000 rpm while still being perfectly comfortable in traffic.

## Stage 2: Deeper Breathing and Fuel — Targeting ~220–250 hp

Stage 2 is where you start getting into the engine a bit more. These mods require more time, more tools, and more careful tuning. You're pushing the naturally aspirated B6304S toward its practical ceiling here.

**Realistic power target: 220–250 hp (depending on how far you go with head work).**

### Head Work — Porting and Polishing

This is the heart of Stage 2. The factory cylinder head has conservative port shapes and sizes. Opening them up lets significantly more air flow through the engine.

- **Port matching** — Make sure the intake and exhaust ports in the head match the gasket openings and manifold runners. Any mismatch creates turbulence and restriction.
- **Bowl blending** — Smooth the transition area around the valve seats (the "bowl" area). This is where the biggest flow gains are on most heads.
- **Polish the exhaust ports** — Smooth exhaust ports help spent gases exit faster. Intake ports should be left slightly rough (not mirror-polished) to help fuel atomization.
- **Larger valves** — If aftermarket oversized valves are available for the B6304S, they're worth considering. Even a 1mm increase in valve diameter can measurably improve flow.
- **Three-angle valve job** — Have the valve seats cut with a proper three-angle (or five-angle) valve job for optimal sealing and flow.

**DIY note:** You can do basic port matching and bowl blending yourself with a Dremel, carbide burrs, and cartridge rolls. Take your time, go slow, and don't remove too much material. If you've never done head work before, practice on a junk head first.

### Upgraded Fuel System

More air needs more fuel. The stock fuel system will start to run lean if you've done significant head work and cam upgrades.

- **High-flow fuel injectors** — Upgrade to injectors with ~20% more capacity than stock. Bosch injectors from turbocharged Volvo applications (like the B6304T2) can sometimes be adapted.
- **Upgraded fuel pump** — A higher-flow in-tank pump ensures consistent fuel pressure under load.
- **Adjustable fuel pressure regulator** — Lets you fine-tune fuel pressure to match your injectors and tune.

### More Aggressive Camshaft

If you went with a mild fast-road cam at Stage 1, Stage 2 is where you step up to something more aggressive.

- Longer duration and higher lift than the Stage 1 cam
- You'll notice a lumpier idle and less low-RPM tractability — this is the trade-off
- **Must be tuned on a dyno or rolling road** — don't just bolt in a cam and hope for the best. The ECU needs to be adjusted to match.

### Upgraded Intake Manifold

If an aftermarket or ported intake manifold is available, this is the time. Larger, smoother runners matched to your ported head will maximize the airflow improvements.

Alternatively, you can port and match the stock intake manifold yourself — clean up the runners, match them to the head ports, and smooth any casting imperfections.

### Full Exhaust Optimization

- **Tubular exhaust header** (if not done at Stage 1) — equal-length runners tuned for the B6304S firing order will improve exhaust scavenging
- **High-flow sports catalyst**
- **Optimized 2.5-inch cat-back** with minimal bends and restrictions

### ECU Tuning (Mandatory at This Stage)

With head work, bigger cams, and upgraded fuel delivery, you absolutely need a proper tune. A standalone ECU (Megasquirt, Apexi, etc.) is strongly recommended at this point. The stock Motronic ECU simply can't adapt to this level of modification.

A good tuner will optimize:
- Fuel maps across the entire RPM range
- Ignition timing advance (safely extracting every last degree)
- Rev limit (the stock limit is conservative)
- Cold start and warm-up enrichment

### Supporting Mods

- **Upgraded clutch/torque converter** — The stock 4-speed auto's torque converter may start to slip with the added power. A rebuilt converter with a higher stall speed can help put power down.
- **Transmission cooler** — Added power means added heat in the transmission. An auxiliary cooler is cheap insurance.
- **Engine oil cooler** — Consider adding one if you're pushing the engine hard, especially in warm climates.

**After Stage 2, you're in the 220–250 hp range with a much broader, more aggressive power band.** The car will pull noticeably harder from 3,500 rpm to redline. It's still naturally aspirated, still reliable for daily driving, but it's starting to feel like a genuinely quick car.

## Stage 3: Forced Induction — 280 hp and Beyond

This is where the B6304S goes from "surprisingly quick" to "genuinely fast." Stage 3 means adding forced induction — a turbocharger or supercharger — and building the engine internals to handle it. This is a major project. We're talking weeks or months of work, significant investment, and a car that may be down for a while. But the results are worth it.

**Realistic power target: 280–350+ hp (depending on boost levels and internal work).**

### The Turbo Route (Most Common)

Adding a turbocharger to the naturally aspirated B6304S is the most popular path to big power. The White Block engine family includes factory-turbocharged variants (like the B6304T2 found in later S80s), which means turbo-specific parts exist within the Volvo ecosystem.

**What you need for a turbo conversion:**

- **Turbocharger** — A Garrett GT28 or GT30-series turbo is a popular choice for this displacement. The GT2860RS is a good balance of quick spool and top-end flow for a 2.9L six. Some builders source the factory Volvo TD04 or Mitsubishi units from turbocharged Volvo donors.
- **Custom exhaust manifold** — You'll need a tubular manifold with a turbo flange. This is typically custom-fabricated or sourced from specialty Volvo tuners. Cast iron log-style manifolds are more durable for daily driving; tubular equal-length manifolds spool faster.
- **Wastegate** — External wastegate (Tial 38mm or similar) for precise boost control, or an internal wastegate if your turbo has one.
- **Intercooler** — A front-mount intercooler (FMIC) is the way to go. Size it appropriately — you don't need a massive drag-racing unit. A core around 24x12x3 inches is plenty for 300+ hp on a street car.
- **Charge piping** — Custom aluminum or silicone piping from the turbo to the intercooler to the throttle body.
- **Blow-off valve or bypass valve** — Protects the turbo from compressor surge on throttle lift.
- **Oil feed and return lines** — The turbo needs oil for its bearings. Tap into the oil pressure sending unit for the feed; weld a bung into the oil pan for the return (gravity drain, must be above oil level).
- **Coolant lines** — Many turbos are water-cooled. Tap into the cooling system for feed and return.
- **Upgraded fuel system** — Larger injectors (550cc+ for 300 hp target), higher-flow fuel pump, adjustable fuel pressure regulator.
- **Standalone ECU** — Absolutely mandatory. Megasquirt, Haltech, or similar. You need full control over boost-dependent fuel and ignition maps, plus knock detection and protection.
- **Wideband O2 sensor and gauge** — For tuning and monitoring air-fuel ratios in real time. Non-negotiable.
- **Boost controller** — Manual boost controller to start (cheap and reliable), or an electronic boost controller for more precise control.

### Internal Engine Strengthening

At boost levels above 5–8 psi on the stock B6304S bottom end, you're rolling the dice. For a reliable 300+ hp daily driver, you want to reinforce the internals.

- **Lower compression pistons** — Forged pistons with a lower compression ratio (around 8.5:1 instead of the stock ~10.7:1) allow you to run more boost safely without detonation.
- **Forged connecting rods** — The stock rods are adequate to around 280–300 hp by most accounts, but forged rods give you a safety margin for higher boost.
- **Head gasket upgrade** — A multi-layer steel (MLS) head gasket handles boost better than the stock gasket.
- **ARP head studs** — Replace the factory head bolts with ARP studs for consistent, higher clamping force. This prevents head gasket failure under boost.
- **Engine balancing and blueprinting** — While the engine is apart, have the rotating assembly balanced. This reduces vibration and stress at high RPM under boost.

### The Supercharger Alternative

A supercharger (Rotrex centrifugal or Eaton/Magnuson roots-type) is another option. Advantages over a turbo:

- No exhaust manifold fabrication needed
- Linear power delivery with no turbo lag
- Simpler plumbing (no wastegate, no BOV needed on most setups)
- Belt-driven off the crankshaft — mechanically simpler

Disadvantages:
- Parasitic power loss (the engine drives the supercharger)
- Generally less peak power potential than a turbo at the same boost level
- Fewer off-the-shelf kits for the B6304S

A Rotrex centrifugal supercharger at 6–8 psi can put you in the 260–300 hp range without touching the internals, making it a compelling option if you want forced induction with less fabrication.

### Drivetrain Upgrades (Mandatory at Stage 3)

The stock AW30-43 4-speed automatic is the weak link at this power level. Options:

- **Rebuilt and strengthened automatic** — A transmission shop can rebuild the AW30-43 with upgraded clutch packs, a higher-stall torque converter, and a shift kit for firmer, faster shifts. This is good to around 300–350 hp.
- **Manual transmission swap** — The Getrag 260 5-speed manual from the 960/S90 (rare but they exist) or an adapter to fit a T5 or M90 gearbox. This is a significant project but gives you full control and better power handling.
- **Upgraded differential** — The stock limited-slip (if equipped) or open diff may need attention. A welded diff is cheap but harsh for daily driving. A proper LSD (Quaife ATB or similar) is the right move.

### Cooling and Oiling

- **Upgraded radiator** — Aluminum performance radiator with more core area
- **Oil cooler** — Mandatory with forced induction. A sandwich plate adapter and remote oil cooler keep oil temps in check.
- **Upgraded oil pump** — Consider this if you're doing a full bottom-end build
- **Catch can** — A PCV catch can prevents oil vapor from entering the intake, which is especially important with a turbo

### Suspension for the Power

At 300+ hp through the rear wheels, the stock suspension geometry and compliance bushings won't cut it.

- **Coilovers** — Adjustable ride height and damping. Look for kits designed for the 960/S90 platform (they exist but are niche).
- **Upgraded sway bars** — IPD makes sway bar kits for the 960 platform. Thicker bars reduce body roll significantly.
- **Polyurethane bushings throughout** — Replace every rubber bushing in the suspension with poly. This tightens up the chassis response dramatically.
- **Subframe reinforcement** — Inspect and reinforce the rear subframe mounts. The multilink rear can stress these under hard launches.
- **Upgraded brakes** — Larger rotors, multi-piston calipers (Brembo or Wilwood kits), braided lines, and high-temp brake fluid (DOT 4 racing). You need to be able to stop as well as you can go.

**After Stage 3, you're looking at 280–350+ hp through the rear wheels of a 3,400-lb sedan.** That's genuinely fast — mid-13-second quarter-mile territory or better. And it still looks like a car your accountant would drive. Peak sleeper status.

## Final Thoughts

The 1997 Volvo S90 is an underrated platform. The B6304S inline-six is smooth, durable, and part of a modular engine family that shares DNA with turbocharged variants making 280+ hp from the factory. The rear-wheel-drive layout means power goes to the correct wheels. And the car itself is comfortable, safe, and looks like it belongs in a bank parking lot — not at a drag strip.

Take your time with Stage 0. Get the maintenance right. Build on a solid foundation. Then work through the stages at whatever pace your budget and garage time allow. Every mod on this list can be done at home with hand tools, a jack, and some patience.

Welcome to the 960/S90 community. These cars reward the people who take care of them.
